Solving Inequalities

Inequality

A sentence that contains > (is greater than), < (is less than), > (is greater than or equal to), < (is less than or equal to), or # (is not equal to). Such sentences are of the following forms:

  • a > b a is greater than b
  • a < b a is less than b
  • a > b a is greater than or equal to b
  • a < b a is less than or equal to b
  • a # b a is not equal to b

    Examples of inequalities that are true statements
    7 > 3 6 < 8 4 > 4 7 < 9 2 # 1

Combining Inequalities: Conjunction

Solve and graph the solution of the conjunction:
x > 3 and x > -2

Numbers that are greater than 3 are also greater than -2. So, the solution set of the conjunction x > 3 and x > -2 is the intersection of the two sets described by the two inequalities.


Conjunctions must be joined by "and" as you see above, or written together. This one could be written as -2< x >3. Note that the direction of the inequality sign is changed when the x is written on the right.

Conjunctions also represent intersections or answers that are true for both inequalities. When graphed, the only solutions are where both lines are found. Above the solutions are represented by the red line only.

Inequalities are solved just like equations. The graphing may be the only material new to you. There is always one exception, though! See below to figure out the exception!

Solve and graph the solution set: -3x < 6

(-3x) + -3 > 6 + -3 Divide each side by -3
x > -2 Multiplying or dividing each side of an inequality by a negative number reverses the inequality sign


Did you figure it out? You must be careful of this one exception to solving inequalities: "When multiplying or dividing each side of an inequality by a negative number, reverse the inequality sign!"

Note that the dot is solid. That is true for inequalities of the form > or <, because -2 is a solution. Inequalities of the form > or < are represented by a hollow circle.

Combining Inequalities: Disjunction

Solve and graph the solution set of the disjunction:

x > 3 or x < 1

Numbers that are greater than 3 or less than 1 are in the solution set. So, the solution set of the disjunction x > 3 or x < 1 is the union of the two sets described by the two inequalities.

Disjunctions must be joined by "or" as you see above.

Disjunctions also represent unions or answers that are true for either inequality. When graphed, the solutions are where either line is found. Above the solutions are represented by the red lines.

Note that the dots here are open. The inequalities are of the form > or <. The numbers 1 & 3 are not included as solutions, so the dot leaves those numbers open.
