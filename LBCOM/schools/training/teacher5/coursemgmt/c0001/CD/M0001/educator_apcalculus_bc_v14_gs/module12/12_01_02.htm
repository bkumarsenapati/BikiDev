Exam Security

The multiple choice section of the exam is released to the public every five years or so, about two years after the administration of that exam. This is because some multiple choice questions are reused each year as part of the grade-equating process. The College Board uses these questions to measure the ability of each year's group of test takers so that there is a high likelihood that a student would earn the same score no matter which year he or she took the exam. For this reason, you must not discuss the content of the multiple choice questions with other students or your teacher. The most recent released exam that we have is from 2008.

Section II

Following the multiple choice calculator-active portion of the exam, you will have a 10-minute break before Section II of the exam begins. Section II is the free response part of the exam, and it also consists of two parts, A and B. The format of the free response section changed in May 2011.

You will answer two free response questions in Part A; some parts require a calculator. You have 30 minutes to complete these two questions, and after that, you must put away your calculator. You will then open Part B of Section II which has four more free response questions, for which a calculator is not needed or permitted. You have 60 minutes to complete these questions.

If you finish Part B before time is called, you may go back to Part A and continue working—but you will not be permitted to use your calculator. We will discuss the free response section in more detail later in this module.

  • Should You Guess on the Multiple Choice Sections?

    Remember that some multiple choice questions will be difficult.

    However, since there is no penalty for guessing, you should eliminate any of the answer choices that you can and then guess from the remaining choices. Do not leave any questions unanswered!
  • Format of Answer Choices

    There are always five answer choices for each question, A–E, and numerical answer choices are always listed from most negative to most positive. "None of these" is rarely a choice.
