7.03 Factoring by Grouping

Now that you have mastered factoring out the GCF and factoring binomials, we are ready to move on in the flow chart.

In this lesson, we will learn what to do when there are four terms in the polynomial. We are going to skip trinomials for now, we will learn about them later.


Step 1
Factor out the Greatest Common Factor
Step 2
Determine which factoring method to use based on the number of terms

Binomial

Is it a difference of squares?

a2 - b2 = (a + b)(a - b)

Trinomial

Does it look like x2 + bx + c

or like ax2 + bx + c?

4-Term Polynomial

Can you factor it by grouping?

 

Step 3
Check to see that nothing can be factored further.
Factoring by Grouping

Just like its name sounds, factoring by grouping requires us to break a polynomial into groups. Here are the steps involved in factoring by grouping. (Before you follow these steps, you must ALWAYS check to see if there is a GCF for the entire polynomial.)

  1. Break the 4 terms into groups of two.
  2. Factor the GCF out of the first two terms.
  3. Factor the GCF out of the last two terms.
  4. If the parentheses for each group match, you can think of the parentheses as another GCF and factor it out of the two groups.

 Example 1

 7x3 - 28x2 + 3x - 12

7x3 - 28x2      + 3x - 12

Step 1: Break the polynomial into two groups. Notice how the sign before the 3x travels with the term.
7x2(x - 4)         Step 2: Factor the GCF out of the first two terms.
                       + 3(x - 4) Step 3: Factor the GCF out of the last two terms.


Step 4: IF the parentheses match, you can factor the parentheses out as another GCF for the entire polynomial.

Notice how the 7x2 that was in front of the first (x -4) and the +3 that was in front of the second (x-4) come together to form (7x2 + 3).


 Example 2

6x3 + 3x2 - 2x - 1

6x3 + 3x2          - 2x - 1

 Step 1: Break the polynomial into two groups. Notice how the sign before the 2x travels with the term.
3x2(2x + 1)        Step 2: Factor the GCF out of the first two terms.
                       - 1(2x + 1) 


 Step 3: Factor the GCF out of the last two terms. Notice the only thing these two terms had in common was their negative sign. When you factor that out, you may want to write it as a -1 in front.

= 3x2(2x + 1) - 1(2x + 1) 

= (2x + 1)(3x2 - 1)

Step 4: IF the parentheses match, you can factor the parentheses out as another GCF for the entire polynomial.

 Example 3

  60ab - 20bx - 30ax + 10x2

= 10(6ab - 2bx - 3ax + x2)

10[6ab - 2bx      - 3ax + x2]

There is a GCF for this polynomial. Notice each term is divisible by 10. Factor this out before starting your factor by grouping process.

Step 1: Break the polynomial into two groups. Notice how the sign before the 3ax travels with the term.

      2b(3a - x) Step 2: Factor the GCF out of the first two terms.
                           - x(3a - x)  

 

 

 Step 3: Factor the GCF out of the last two terms. Notice the 3ax is negative but the x2 is not. If you factored a positive x out of each of these, the signs in the two sets of parentheses would NOT match. Therefore, factor a negative x out of each and you will be able to continue in the factoring process.

= 10[ 2b(3a - x) - x(3a - x) ]

= 10 (3a - x)(2b - x)

Step 4: IF the parentheses match, you can factor the parentheses out as another GCF for the entire polynomial.

Are you wondering what happens if the parentheses don't match? What do you do?

There are two possible reasons for the parentheses not to match in this process:

  1. There is an error in your selection of the GCF or in the process of factoring out the GCF.
  2. The polynomial is not factorable. In this case, you would write prime if no factoring is possible.
