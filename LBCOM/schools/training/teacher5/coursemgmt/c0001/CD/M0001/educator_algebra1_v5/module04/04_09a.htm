04.09 Variation

We have been working on solving equations, but did you know that equations also show relationships? Unless you know what you are looking for, the relationship is often hidden.

Where do you see these relationships in a job situation?

What if a person working for the National Park system as a park ranger needed to tranquilize an animal in order to move it to another location in the park? The amount of sedative used must vary with the size and weight of the animal and the time required to move the animal. The distance the ranger must be from the animal depends on a number of factors including how dangerous the animal may be. This situation can be represented by equations that indicate relationships between variables.

Direct Variation

Direct Variation means that the values of x and y are either both increasing or both decreasing.

Let's write an equation for the gross sales of a music store where the average sale per customer is $4.

C is the number of customers that enter the store
S is the amount of sales
S = $4C

What happens to the sales as the number of customers increases?

What happens to the sales as the number of customers decreases?

The relationship between the sales and the number of customers is a direct variation because the sales is directly related to how many customers enter the store. As the number of customers increases, the amount of sales increases.

Inverse Variation

Inverse Variation means that as the value of x increases, the value of y decreases or vice versa.

Let's write an equation for the monthly payment on a car loan where the price of the car is $20,000.

M is the number of months to pay off the loan (# of payments)
P is the amount of each payment
PM = $20,000

What happens to the amount of each payment as the number of months increases?
(Think: The more time you take to pay the loan, the less you will pay each month.)


What happens to the amount of each payment as the number of months decreases?
(Think: The less time you take to pay the loan, the more you will pay each month)


The relationship between time to pay the loan and amount you pay each month is an inverse relationship because the amount you pay each month is inversely related to the time it takes to pay off the loan. As your time increases, your payments decrease.

Two Examples of Direct Variation


The two examples above show patterns and trends where there is a relationship between x and y.
Equations also show patterns and trends.

Below are some examples of Direct Variation problems. Often these direct variation equations are called proportions.

A direct variation is described by an equation of the form y = kx.

The letter k represents the constant of variation (k is always the same in a problem...constant).
These problems are normally written as proportions and are seen throughout all mathematics courses and on the job.

Example 1:

If y varies directly as x,
and y = 28 when x = 7,
find x when y = 52.

For direct variations you can write a proportion.


Example 2:

The ratio of foreign students to American students at a college is 2 to 32. How many foreign students attend this college if there are 1792 American students?

First write the ratio in words.

Next write the numbers that go with the words, keeping the numbers in the same position in the ratio as the words. Notice that the numbers relating to foreign students are always in the numerator, and the numbers relating to the Americans are always in the denominator.

Now solve as shown in the previous example.
