Factoring Trinomials of the form x2 + bx + c.

In this lesson, we are going to focus on factoring trinomials that are of the form x2 + bx + c .
Note: the variables "b" and "c" are representing coefficients (numbers).

Here are some important points about these trinomials.

  • These trinomials start off with a variable. There is no coefficient (number) in front of the x2.
  • Notice how the powers of x descend. That means they work down from x2 to the last term having no x at all.
  • Sometimes these trinomials start with powers of x higher than x2... like x4, or x6.
  • Sometimes these trinomials contain two variables. Usually, the first variable will be descending (going from high powers down to lower powers) and the second variable will be ascending (going from a lower power up to a higher power). Here is an example of that type of trinomial.
    x2 + 2xy + y2     See how the x's descend and the y's ascend?

Factoring these trinomials is like working the FOIL process in reverse.

On the left are some examples of binomials that have been multiplied using the FOIL process. On the right are the corresponding factored expressions. Look at how the FOIL (multiplication) matches with the Factoring (division). They are opposite functions.

Now you see the relationship, but how do you factor trinomials of the form x2 + bx + c?

Factoring these trinomials is sort of like "guessing and checking". You can use the information in the trinomial to help you guess and then use the FOIL method to check to see if you have guessed correctly. Remember, when you factor a trinomial, it should always multiply back to what you started with.

The first three examples below deal with different situations that occur when factoring trinomials in the form x2 + bx + c . The different situations are:

  1. All terms in the trinomial are positive, x2 + bx + c .
  2. The middle term in the trinomial is negative and the last term is positive, x2 - bx + c .
  3. The middle and last terms in the trinomial are both negative, x2 - bx - c .

The fourth example shows what to do when there is more than one variable in the problem.

 Example 1: All terms in the trinomial are positive, x2 + bx + c.

x2 + 6x + 8

Step 1: Identify all the factor pairs for the last term in the trinomial. (Factor pairs are numbers that multiply to make the last number.)

Step 2: Select the pair of factors that will also add up to the middle term in the trinomial, 6x.
=(x + 2)(x + 4) Step 3: Knowing the first term in each parentheses set must multiply to make x2, place an x in the first position of each parentheses. Knowing the last term in each parentheses set must multiply to make 8 and add to 6, use 2 and 4 in the parentheses.

Check:

(x + 2)(x + 4) = x2 + 4x +2x + 8
                       = x2 + 6x + 8

Step 4: Check your guess by using the FOIL process to verify that the factored answer does multiply back to x2 + 6x + 8 .

 Example 2: The middle term in the trinomial is negative and the last term is positive, x2 - bx + c.

x2 - 12x + 35

Step 1: Identify all the factor pairs for the last term in the trinomial.

Step 2: Select the pair of factors that will also add up to the middle term in the trinomial, -12x.

=(x - 5)(x - 7) Step 3: Since the middle term is negative and the last term is positive, both signs in your factored answer will be negative. Knowing the last term in each parentheses set must multiply to make +35 and add to -12, use -5 and -7 in the parentheses.

Check:

(x - 5)(x - 7) = x2 - 7x - 5x + 35
                       = x2 - 12x + 35

Step 4: Check your guess by using the FOIL process to verify that the factored answer does multiply back to x2 - 12x + 35 .

 Example 3: The middle and last terms in the trinomial are both negative, x2 - bx - c.

x2 - 3x - 18

Step 1: Identify all the factor pairs for the last term in the trinomial.

Step 2: Select the pair of factors that will also add up to the middle term in the trinomial, -3x.

=(x + 3)(x - 6) Step 3: Since the middle and the last terms are both negative, you will need one positive and one negative in your factored parentheses. Knowing the last term in each parentheses set must multiply to make -18 and add to make -3, use 3 and -6 in the parentheses.

Check:

(x + 3)(x - 6) = x2 -6x + 3x - 18
                       = x2 -3x - 18

Step 4: Check your guess by using the FOIL process to verify that the factored answer does multiply back to x2 -3x - 18 .

 Example 4: There is more than one variable in the problem.

x2 +2xy + y2

Step 1: Identify all the factor pairs for the last term in the trinomial.

Step 2: Select the pair of factors that will also add up to the middle term in the trinomial, 2xy.
=(x + 1y)(x + 1y) Step 3: Knowing the last term in each parentheses set must multiply to make 1y2 and the coefficients must add up to 2, place 1y in each parentheses.

Check:

(x + y)(x + y) = x2 +1xy + 1xy + y2
                       = x2 +2xy + y2

Step 4: Check your guess by using the FOIL process to verify that the factored answer does multiply back to x2 +2xy + y2 .

You can determine the signs for the parentheses in the factored answer before you begin your guess and check process. The signs follow a pattern based on the signs in the original trinomial. Here are the sign rules.

Sign rules for Factoring Trinomials:

  • If the last term in the trinomial is positive, the signs in the factored answer will be the same.
    • If the middle term is positive, the signs in the factored answer will both be positive.
    • If the middle term is negative, the signs in the factored answer will both be negative.
  • If the last term in the trinomial is negative, the signs in the factored answer will be different.
