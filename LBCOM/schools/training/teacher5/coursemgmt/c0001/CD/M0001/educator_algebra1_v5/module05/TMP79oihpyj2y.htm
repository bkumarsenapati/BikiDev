05.11 Solving Systems of Equations

Now we will learn to solve two linear equations at the same time. If you read the lesson carefully and do the practice problems, you'll see that it's not as hard as it sounds...promise!

Solving Systems of Two Equations

A system of two equations is created when you have two equations and try to find a solution that works for both equations. For example, here is the situation we mentioned in the opening of this session:

Suppose we want to find two numbers. We know the sum of one number and twice a second number is 1 and their difference is 4.

Let's let x = first number and let y = the second number

1) We know their sum of one number and twice the second number is 1
2) We know their difference is 4

Equation 1 : x +2y = 1
Equation 2: x - y = 4

These two equations make a system of equations. We want to know two numbers (an x value and a y value) that would make both of these equations true (i.e. meeting two conditions at the same time).


When you solve a system of equations, you are looking for a solution set (ordered pair) that works in both equations . You can find this ordered pair in one of three ways. Over the next lessons, we will be working with the several methods for solving a system of equations. The methods are:

  1. Graphing method
  2. Addition/Subtraction method
  3. Multiplication method
  4. Substitution method

Solving Systems of Equations Using the Graphing Method

To help you understand the concept of a solution to a system of equations, we will solve the system of equations from above by using the graphing method.

We are looking for a point that is common to both equations so we are going to graph the equations on the same coordinate plane and find the point of intersection . The point of intersection will be an ordered pair that is a solution to both equations and therefore the solution to our system of equations.

Graph the first equation : x + 2y = 1

1) identify the slope and the y intercept. We know the easiest way to find it is by putting the equation in slope intercept form (y = mx + b).

2) x + 2y = 1 [solve for y: add - x to both sides of the equation and divide all terms by 2]
3) y = - x +

So the slope will be - and the y-intercept will be the point (0, ).
To graph the line, you need to go to the y intercept and then count down 1 and to the right 2 to get another point on the line. Remember the slope is the change in y over the change in x so -1 over 2 means down 1 and to the right 2.


Graph the second equation: x - y = 4

1) Identify the slope and y-intercept. Begin by putting the equation in slope
intercept form.

2) x - y = 4 [solve for y: add -x to both sides and divide by -1]

3) y = x - 4

So the slope is 1 and the y-intercept is -4 or (0, -4) . To graph the line, you need to go to the y-intercept and then count up 1 and to the right 1 to find another point on the line.


Here are the two graphs on the same coordinate plane.

 

The point (3,-1) is the point of intersection and therefore the solution to the system of equations. This means that the ordered pair (3, -1) will work in both equations.


Let's check the answer in each equation.

We are checking the point (3, -1) so x = 3 and y = -1 in BOTH EQUATIONS

Equation 1: x + 2y = 1

3 + 2(-1) = 1
3 -2 = 1
1 = 1
This is true so the point works in this equation.

Equation 2: x - y = 4

3 - (-1) = 4
3 + 1 = 4
4 = 4

This is true so the point works in this equation.

(3, -1) is the solution to the system of equations.
