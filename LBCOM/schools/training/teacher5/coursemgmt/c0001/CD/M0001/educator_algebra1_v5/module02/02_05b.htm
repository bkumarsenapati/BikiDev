02.05 Lab Assignment

Before proceeding you will need to do the following:
    1. Retrieve the data table file you created in 2.01
    2. Get a calculator (only if you want to use it to do calculations)
    3. Download the Algebra Lab Report in .rtf format. Note that when you click on this, you should save it into your Algebra folder that you created back in assignment 02.01.

Let's see how you and your data buddies come out on the 'to be a square or rectangle' question. As stated on the previous page, if the ratio of ones arm span to height is 1 to 1 or close to it, you are considered a square. If the ratio is not close to 1 to 1, you are a rectangle.

Step 1: Without looking closely at the data, predict the ratio of square to rectangles in your data sample (you and the nine classmates). In science language, this prediction is really an hypothesis.

TO DO: Write the prediction and a reason why you made the prediction you did.

Step 2: Figure out the arm span to height ratios for all ten data sets. Figure out how close the ratio must be to 1 to 1 for you to consider someone a square or a rectangle?

TO DO: Make a new column on your data table and call it Ratios. Put the arm span to height ratios of the students in the appropriate cells. Use any of the ratio notations such as "3:2", "3:2", or "3 to 2". Be sure to save your table.

Step 3: Determine whether each student is a square or a rectangle.

TO DO: Make another column on the table and called it Shape. Enter in square or rectangle in the appropriate cells for each student.

Step 4: Find the ratio of squares to rectangles in your data sample.

TO DO: Describe the process you follow to find the ratio of squares to rectangles. Record your answer and show the work you did to find the answer.

Step 5: Compare your hypothesis (prediction) to the calculated ratio (the one you just figured out).

TO DO: Write some sentences discussing the results of the comparison. For example, Did you guess the ratio exactly? Were you close? Off by how much? Way off? Surprised by the results?

Are we having fun yet?

Let's do some more with this data and see if we can understand what these statistics really mean.

Step 6: Find the mode of the arm spans in your data sample.

TO DO: Record the mode. Explain what your mode means in terms of all the arm spans in your data sample.

Step 7: Find the mean (or average) of the heights of the students in your data sample.
TO DO: Record the mean. Explain what the mean means in terms of all the heights in your data sample.

Helpful Resource : http://www.mste.uiuc.edu/hill/dstat/meanbball.html

Step 8: Find the median of the heights of the students in your data sample.

TO DO: Record the median. Explain what the median means in terms of all the heights in your data sample.
Helpful Resource : http://www.mste.uiuc.edu/hill/dstat/medianbball.html

Step 9: TO DO: If you were to draw a graph to show the number of male squares, female squares, male rectangles and female rectangles in your data sample, what type of graph would you use and why? Remember - you do not have to draw the graph!
