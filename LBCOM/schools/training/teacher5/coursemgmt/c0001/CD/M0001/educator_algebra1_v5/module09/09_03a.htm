09.03 Graphing

In the last lesson, you learned to solve a single inequality. Real life usually involves a combination of inequalities.

  • Remember the resistors within certain tolerances?
  • What about a doctor that wants his/her patient to have a blood pressure reading and a cholesterol reading within a safe range to prevent heart disease?

These are called combined inequalities! In this lesson, you will learn how to find the solutions to combined inequalities.

There are two types of combined inequalities, conjunctions and disjunctions. Read about each type below and see how to solve and graph them.


Conjunctions

What does the word "Conjunction" mean?

In Algebra, conjunction refers to the combining of math sentences with the word " and ." Some refer to this as an intersection of two or more sentences.

Example 1

x > -3 and x < 2

This is an example of a conjunction because of the word AND that joins the two inequalities. This means that all solutions of the combined inequality must be greater than -3 AND less than 2. Can you think of some numbers that would fit that description?

Graphing the solution

We are going to graph the solutions to the combined inequality. Here are the steps and a picture of the graph.

  1. Graph each inequality above a number line. The inequality x > -3 is graphed above the line in red . The inequality x < 2 is graphed above the line in blue .
  2. Look for the place where the two graphs intersect each other. The "intersection" of the inequalities is the solution to the conjunction. The solutions to this combined inequality are graphed in purple .

Example 2

-11 <= -4x + 1 < 17

This one looks a little weird! Often an "and" statement will be written all together as you see here. There are actually two inequalities.

-11 <= -4x + 1 AND -4x + 1 < 17

We will have to solve the inequalities before we can graph the solutions to the conjunction.

-4x + 1 >= -11
(I changed the order of the first inequality to make it easier to solve.)

-4x + 1 - 1 >= -11 - 1
-4x >= -12
x <= 3

Notice that the inequality sign changed when we divided both sides by -4.

-4x + 1 < 17

-4x + 1 - 1 < 17 - 1
-4x < 16
x > -4

Notice that the inequality sign changed when we divided both sides by -4.

Graphing the solution

We are going to graph the solutions to the combined inequality. Here are the steps and a picture of the graph.

  1. Graph each inequality above a number line. The inequality x <= 3 is graphed above the line in red . The inequality x > -4 is graphed above the line in blue .
  2. Look for the place where the two graphs intersect each other. The "intersection" of the inequalities is the solution to the conjunction. The solutions to this combined inequality are graphed in purple .


Disjunctions

What does the word "Disjunction" mean?

In Algebra, disjunction refers to the combining of math sentences with the word " or ." Some refer to this as a union of two or more sentences.

Example 1

x > 5 or x < 1

This is an example of a disjunction because of the word OR that joins the two inequalities. This means that all solutions of the combined inequality can be greater than 5 OR less than 1. Can you think of some numbers that would fit that description?


Graphing the solution

We are going to graph the solutions to the combined inequality. Here are the steps and a picture of the graph.

  1. Graph each inequality above a number line. The inequality x > 5 is graphed above the line in red . The inequality x < 1 is graphed above the line in blue .
  2. Look for the solutions that fit either graph. They do not have to intersect but instead you will take all the solutions from both graphs. The "union" of the inequalities is the solution to the disjunction. The solutions to this combined inequality are graphed in purple .

Example 2

2x + 3 >= 9 or -3x >= -6

We will have to solve the inequalities before we can graph the solutions to the disjunction.


2x + 3 >= 9

2x + 3 - 3 >= 9 - 3
2x >= 6
x >= 3

 


-3x >= -6

-3x >= -6
x <= 2

Notice that the inequality sign changed when we divided both sides by -3.

Graphing the solution

We are going to graph the solutions to the combined inequality. Here are the steps and a picture of the graph.

  1. Graph each inequality above a number line. The inequality x >= 3 is graphed above the line in red . The inequality x <= 2 is graphed above the line in blue .
  2. Look for the solutions that fit either graph. They do not have to intersect but instead you will take all the solutions from both graphs. The "union" of the inequalities is the solution to the disjunction. The solutions to this combined inequality are graphed in purple.
