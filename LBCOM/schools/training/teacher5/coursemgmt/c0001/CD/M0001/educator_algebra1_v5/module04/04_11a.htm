04.11 Functions

Have you ever asked yourself, "What's my role, or what's my function?" at a particular job or responsibility? Seems fitting that much of algebra is dealing with functions too.

Let's say you were a lifeguard for the summer. Your job description contains many parts. You may be required to watch swimmers, post surf conditions, administer first aid, and/or clean the beach. Your actions in your job will bring about various results.

Equations work similarly. When you put a value into an equation, you will get various results. The inputs and outputs of equations are represented by ordered pairs. A set of ordered pairs is called a relation.


Characteristics of Relations and Functions

Relations: A relation is a set of ordered pairs. Remember an ordered pair consists of two values (x, y).
Functions: A function is a relation with specific characteristics. A function is also a set of ordered pairs, but each x value only has one y value.

There are many ways to view relations and functions. They can be written as sets of ordered pairs or you can graph the ordered pairs to create a picture on a coordinate plane.

All of the eight sets of ordered pairs above are examples of relations, but only the four sets of ordered pairs in Group B are functions.

How is Group B different from Group A? What do you notice about the x and y values of the ordered pairs? The answers to these questions will help us to understand the definitions of relations and functions.

Look at the first set of ordered pairs in group A. Do you see the ordered pairs (1,-3) and (1,8)? The x values are the same but they have different y values. This is an indication that the relation is NOT a function.

Look at the sets in Group B. Notice that there are no repeating x values. This is an indication that the relations are functions.

A relation can take on many forms. You can illustrate a relation by writing the set of ordered pairs, graphing the ordered pairs or putting the ordered pairs in a chart.

All relations will have an equation to represent them. The equations will be shown but for now, you only need to understand the difference between a relation and a function and the ways in which the ordered pairs are illustrated.

Question 3: Is the relation R a function?

Now let's look at the graph of relation R.Notice that all the ordered pairs have been put on a coordinate plane and then connected to create the graph.We can also tell whether or not the relation R is a function by looking at the graph. We will use what is called the vertical line test to determine if relation R is a function. Hold your pencil vertically (up and down), and run it across the graph from left to right. Do this now on the graph to the right. Notice that your pencil only touches the graph once no matter where you place your pencil (remember that your pencil must be vertical). When your pencil only touches the graph once, then the graph (or relation) is a function. In example 2 below you will have a different outcome when you run your pencil vertically along the graph. This method is called the Vertical Line Test and is used to tell whether or not a given graph is a function.The x values on the graph cannot have more than one y value to be considered a function.
