09.06 Slope Review

In the first part of our review, you learned about the relationships that exist between linear equations, tables of values, graphs and solutions to equations. In this part of the review, we are going to focus on applying what we know about slope to everyday experiences and finding the slopes of lines.

What is slope?
If you were skateboarding up or down ramps, the steepness of the ramp would determine the difficulty of your run. The steeper the incline or decline, the more experienced you would need to be because you promised your mom you would not hurt yourself! In math, we call the steepness of the incline or decline of a line, the SLOPE.

Slope tells us the steepness of the line. Slope compares how fast a line goes up (incline) and down (decline) with how fast the line goes right and left. The bigger the slope, the steeper the line. The smaller the slope, the flatter the line.

Try this: Suppose you have a friend who is afraid of heights. You want this friend to overcome this fear so they will go on the really steep water slide with you. You decide to go on a less scary (less steep) slide first.

Slide 1 has a slope of 5.
Slide 2 has a slope of 2.
Slide 3 has a slope of 6.

Which slide is the least scary? Why?
Which is the scariest? Why?

Answers:
Slide 2 is the least scary because the slope is only 2, the smallest of the given slopes.
Slide 3 is the scariest because the slope is 6, and it is bigger than the other slopes.

The negative sign in front of the 6 tells us whether the line is rising or falling. It does not affect the steepness of the slope. In other words, when we compare slopes we are comparing the absolute values of the slopes.

Method # 2: The slope can be found if we have the graph of the line and can find the coordinates of two points that are on the line.

Given any two points on a line, we can think of slope as the amount the y coordinates change in relation to the amount the x coordinates change.

We call the change of y the RISE (because the y values go in the vertical direction or UP) and the change of x the RUN (because the x values go in the horizontal direction or ACROSS) .

Slopes of Horizontal and Vertical Lines:

In the last review section we worked with graphs of horizontal and vertical lines. Now, we will take what we have learned and learn determine the slopes of these lines.
Horizontal Lines:


Example:

y = 3

Table of Values:

Graph:

Slope: Since we have a table of points that we know are on the line y = 3, let's pick any two points from the table and use them to find the slope. If we selected (-4, 3) and (2, 3) as our points, what is the slope?

Notice the numerator of the slope formula had two identical values being subtracted. 3 - 3 = 0. Take a moment and use two different ordered pairs from our table of values to find the slope. What do you notice? No matter which two points you use, the y values are always 3 and 3 minus 3 will always equal zero.All horizontal lines work this way. They are horizontal because every point on the line has the same y value.

So, we can say that all horizontal lines have a slope of zero.
m = 0.

Vertical Lines:

Example:

x = -2

Table of Values:

Graph:

Slope: Since we have a table of points that we know are on the line x = -2, let's pick any two points from the table and use them to find the slope. If we selected (-2, -3) and (-2, 5) as our points, what is the slope?

    

You cannot divide a number by 0, the result is undefined. Take a moment and use two different ordered pairs from our table of values to find the slope. What do you notice? No matter which two points you use, the x values are always -2 and negative 2 minus negative 2 will always equal zero. All vertical lines work this way. They are vertical because every point on the line has the same x value. So, we say that all vertical lines have an undefined slope. In other words, there is no slope.

This can be confusing for students at first, but imagine yourself skateboarding down a vertical ramp. If you tried it, you would probably be "undefined" when you hit the ground.
