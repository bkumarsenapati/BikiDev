06.12 Lab: Volume of a Cylinder Exploration

Get ready to have some fun!


Directions for the Lab

1. Collect the following materials:

  • Ruler in centimeters
  • empty cans of various sizes with the lids off and cleaned out
  • String
  • Paper and pencil

2. Measure

a. Place one of your two cans face down on your paper and trace the base (the top or bottom circle of the can).

b. Take your string and lay it carefully along the outside of the circle to measure how far around the circle is (the circumference). Once you have measured the circle, put the string on your ruler and measure the length of the string in centimeters.

c. Approximate to the best of your ability the center of the circle and measure the circle's diameter (see picture below) in centimeters.

 

d. Now take your circumference measurement and divide it by your diameter measurement and record that number.

e. Repeat a-d for your second can.

f. Compare your answer for d for each of the two circles. Are the two numbers close?

g. The formula to find the circumference (C) of a circle where d is the diameter of the circle and is the irrational number 3.1415926... is C = d. Now if we solve this equation for , we'll have C/d = . Are your two numbers from d close to 3.14? How close?

h. The formula to find the area (A) of a circle is A = r2 (Here r is the radius of the circle and remember that the radius is half of the diameter). Find the area of each circle. Use 3.14 for on all of your calculations.

i. Measure the height of each can in centimeters.

j. Now let's find the volume (V) of your two cans. Volume is how much each can will hold. The formula for finding the volume of a cylinder is V = (area of base)(height) = (r2 h). Remember that your unit of measure here will be cm since you measured the radius and height in centimeters.

k. Finally, fill each can full of water and measure in cups how much water each can holds. Give a brief statement about how many cups. Make a note of how many cups equals your cubic centimeter volume measurement.
