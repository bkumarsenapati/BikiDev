Wouldn’t it be great if you could gaze into a special mathematical crystal ball that would help you see into the "future of quadratic equations"? It would answer questions for you like, "how many roots (or solutions) will it have?" Since you learned in a previous lesson that the graph of a quadratic equation is a parabola, and that the parabola crosses the x-axis at its roots, you know that this knowledge could help you picture the parabola and once you have a picture, you would know how many solutions to expect.

Let's do some discovering!

Examine the quadratic equations and their graphs.

This parabola is the graph of the equation x2 - 8x + 12.

Since the parabola crosses the x-axis at two places, there are 2 real roots to the quadratic equation.

This parabola is the graph of the equation x2 - 8x + 16.

Since the parabola crosses the x-axis at one place, there is 1 real root to the quadratic equation.

This parabola is the graph of the equation x2 - 4x + 5.

Since the parabola does not cross the x-axis, there are no real roots to the quadratic equation.


Travel Advisory:


Recall from lesson 7.09 that the roots of a quadratic equation(s) can also be referred to as the zeros of the function or the solutions to the equation.

This will be an important concept to remember as you move to higher level math and strive for success on your final exam.

Question: How can we use the discriminant to determine the number of solutions we can expect a quadratic equation to have?

b2 - 4ac is the stuff under the radical sign in the quadratic formula.
