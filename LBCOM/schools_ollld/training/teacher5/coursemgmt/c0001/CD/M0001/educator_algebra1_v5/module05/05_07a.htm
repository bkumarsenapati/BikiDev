05.07 Graphing Linear Equations

Enough with equations, let's get to the pictures.

You are ready to graph linear equations. In this lesson, you will focus on the following.

  • Graphing vertical and horizontal lines.
  • Graphing using the slope intercept form (y = mx + b) of the line.
Converting equations written in standard form (Ax + By = C) to slope intercept form and then graphing them.

Graphing horizontal and vertical lines.

As we reviewed earlier, horizontal and vertical lines have special equations.

Examples of Horizontal Lines: (Notice there are no x values in the equations.)

  • y = -2
  • y = 3
  • y = 0

Examples of Vertical Lines: (Notice there are no y values in the equations.)

  • x = 4
  • x = 0
  • x = -3

Now, let's graph these equations.

To graph a horizontal line, you simply draw a horizontal line that intersects the y axis at the number given in the equation. For example, in y = -2, the line intersects the y axis at -2.

To graph a vertical line, you simply draw a vertical line that intersects the x axis at the number given in the equation. For example, in x = 4, the line intersects the x axis at 4.

Remember, the equation y = -2 has no x-value. This means no matter what you choose as the value for x, y is always -2.

Look at the graph and find all the points where y = -2. Notice there are many points (an infinite amount, actually) but they form a horizontal line.

In this case, the graph is a horizontal line through the number -2 on the y-axis.

The equation y = 3 has no x-value. This means no matter what you choose as the value for x, y is always 3.

Look at the graph and find all the points where y = 3. Did you find a lot of points?

In this case, the graph is a horizontal line through the number 3 on the y-axis.

In this equation, you are asked to graph the line where y is always equal to zero. Do you know another name for the line where the y-values are always zero?

This is a tricky one but if you find all the points where y = 0, the answer is easy.

Answer: The x-axis is the line where y is always zero .

Look at the graph and find all the points where x = -3. Notice there are many points (an infinite amount, actually) but they form a vertical line .

In this case, the graph is a vertical line through the number -3 on the y-axis.

In this equation, you are asked to graph the line where x is always equal to zero. Do you know another name for the line where the x-values are always zero?

Look at the graph and find all the points where x = 0. We bet this is easy for you now!

Answer: The y-axis is the line where x is always zero .

 


Graphing linear equations in the form y = mx + b.

When an equation is written in slope intercept form, y = mx +b, what do m and b represent?

  • m represents the slope of the line
  • b represents the place where the line crosses the y-axis, called the y-intercept

You can graph linear equations by simply knowing the values of m and b.

Example:

Step 1: Identify the slope and the y-intercept.

Step 2:Graph the y-intercept.

In this example, b = -2.

It is important that you know the y-intercept is the ordered pair where x equals 0. If you were to substitute 0 in the equation for "x" and solve for y, your answer would be -2.


 y = 0 - 2
 y = -2

The easy way of doing this is to locate the y intercept on the graph. Name the point. Notice any point on the y axis has an x value of 0 so, in this case, the y intercept is (0, -2)

Step 3: Use the slope to help you graph another point on the line.

Remember, slope is rise over run or the change in y over the change in x. The slope 3/4 tells us to move up 3 units (rise) and to the right 4 units (run).

Starting at the y-intercept, move up 3 units and to the right 4 units and draw another point. The coordinates of this point are (4, 1).

If you want to, you can continue moving up 3 units and to the right 4 units to draw more points on the line. However, having 2 points to connect is enough because it only takes two points to determine a line.

Step 4: Connect the points to make a line.
