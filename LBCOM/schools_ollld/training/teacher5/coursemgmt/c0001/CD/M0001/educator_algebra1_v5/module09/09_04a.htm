09.04 HONORS Graphing

Please skip this assignment if you are not taking the course for Honors credit.

There is a common assumption that all "and" statements graph between the two numbers and all "or" statements graph to the outside of the numbers. Students often assume this fact because it is common for this pattern to occur.

However, this is not a true assumption. You have to focus on the meanings of conjunction and disjunction. Remember that a conjunction (and statement) will graph where the two graphs overlap and a disjunction (or statement) will graph all parts of each graph.

Let's look at some of the more unusual examples of conjunctions and disjunctions.

Disjunctions

Example 1

Consider the combined inequality below.

x < -5 and x > 2

We are going to graph the solutions to the combined inequality. Here are the steps and a picture of the graph.

  1. Graph each inequality above a number line. The inequality x < -5 is graphed above the line in red . The inequality x > 2 is graphed above the line in blue .
  2. Look for the place where the two graphs intersect each other. The "intersection" of the inequalities is the solution to the conjunction. Well here is where we have a problem, the graphs do not intersect. There are not any solutions that would satisfy both inequalities so there are "no solutions" to this conjunction.

Example 2

Consider the combined inequality below.

x > -4 or x < 7

We are going to graph the solutions to the combined inequality. Here are the steps and a picture of the graph.

  1. Graph each inequality above a number line. The inequality x > -4 is graphed above the line in red . The inequality x < 7 is graphed above the line in blue .
  2. Look for the solutions that fit either graph. They do not have to intersect but instead you will take all the solutions from both graphs. The "union" of the inequalities is the solution to the disjunction. Since the solutions of both graphs take up the whole number line, the solutions (in purple ) are "all real numbers."
