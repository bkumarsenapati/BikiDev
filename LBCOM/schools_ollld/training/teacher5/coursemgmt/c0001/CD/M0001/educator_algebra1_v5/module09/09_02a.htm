09.02 Inequalities

In Module 4, you learned how to solve equations. Developing this skill was essential for understanding linear equations and their graphs.

In this module, you will learn about inequalities! An inequality means the value of the variable is not necessary equal to one number but instead may be greater than or less than a certain number. Inequalities are very visual because they usually represent an infinite number of solutions.

What does infinite mean again?

If a company wants to maximize their profits, there would be one product cost that would make that a reality. This means a single solution.

What if the company just wants to make a profit? There are an infinite number of ways to make a profit. Selling more, lowering costs, buying less...! There are many combinations of methods for making the most of your product and therefore making some kind of a profit.

Inequalities have many solutions.


Solutions to inequalities

Just like with equations, the solution to an inequality is a value that makes the inequality true. Let's look at a few examples of inequalities and their solutions.


x < 7

This inequality means that x can be any number that is less than 7. Can you think of some numbers that would be possible values for x? Numbers like 6.5, 6, 5.2, 0, -3 and -5 would all be possible solutions for x.

What about 7? Is it true that 7 is less than 7? Well of course not! 7 is not actually an answer. It is just a reference point for the answers. All the solutions to this inequality are numbers that are less than 7.


x > -5

This inequality means that x can be any number that is greater than or equal to -5. Can you think of some numbers that would be possible values for x? Numbers like -4, -3.5, -1, 0, 7, 8 and 9 would all be possible solutions for x.

What about -5? Is it true that -5 is greater than or equal to -5? Well of course it is! -5 is an answer because of the "equal" sign in the inequality. All the solutions to this inequality are numbers that are greater than or equal to -5.

Solving inequalities

You can solve inequalities in the same way you can solve equations, except for one little difference. First, let's review the parts of solving that are the same.

  • You must keep the inequality balanced.
  • You should do the opposite math operation.

As you learned in lesson 4.04, you can use also the distributive property and collecting like terms to simplify the problem as you are solving for the values of the variable.

Now, what is this "little difference"?

*When you multiply or divide both sides of an inequality by a negative number, you have to reverse the direction of the inequality sign.*


Example 1

x + 4 > -8
x + 4 - 4 > -8 - 4
x > -12

The sign does not reverse because you are not multiplying or dividing in your solving of this inequality.


Example 2

2x + 7 < -11
2x + 7 - 7 < -11 - 7
2x < -18
x < -9

The sign does not reverse because you are not dividing by a negative number.


Example 3

-4x - 1 < -13
-4x - 1 + 1 < -13 + 1
-4x < -12
x > 3

The sign must reverse because you are dividing both sides of the equation by -4.


Example 4

-3(x + 2) + 2x > -9
-3x - 6 + 2x > -9
-1x - 6 > -9
-1x - 6 + 6 > -9 + 6
-1x > -3
x < 3

The sign must reverse because you are dividing both sides of the equation by -1.


Example 5

3(x + 1) < 2(x - 5)
3x + 3 < 2x - 10
3x - 2x + 3 < -10
x + 3 < -10
x + 3 - 3 < -10 - 3
x < -13

The sign does not reverse because you are not multiplying or dividing in your solving of this inequality.


Example 6

-10 > 5(x - 1)
This is weird! The x is on the right. It is probably best to work with the x on the left. It is okay to change the order of things but be sure to change the inequality sign as well

5(x - 1) < -10
5x - 5 < -10
5x - 5 + 5 < -10 + 5
5x < -5
x < -1

The sign does not reverse because you are not dividing by a negative number.

Graphing Inequalities

Since there are an infinite number of solutions to an inequality, graphing makes the solutions come alive! The visual effect of a graph is really helpful when there are many possibilities for an answer.

When you graph an inequality, there are two things to consider.

  • Will you put an open or closed circle on the reference number in the inequality?
  • Will you shade the values to the left or the right of the reference number?


Example 1

x > -12

Remember that our goal is to graph the solutions to this inequality. The values of x that work in the inequality are numbers that are greater than -12, however -12 is NOT a solution because there is not an "equal" sign as part of the inequality.

  • Open circle on the -12
  • Shade to the right of -12


Example 2

x < -9

The values of x that work in the inequality are less than or equal to -9. In this case, the -9 IS a solution because there is an "equal" sign as part of the inequality.

  • Closed circle on the -9
  • Shade to the left of -9


Example 3

x < 3

The values of x that work in the inequality are numbers that are less than 3, however 3 is NOT a solution because there is not an "equal" sign as part of the inequality.

  • Open circle on the 3
  • Shade to the left of 3
