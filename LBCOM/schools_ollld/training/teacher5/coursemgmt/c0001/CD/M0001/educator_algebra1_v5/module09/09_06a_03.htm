09.06 Graphing Linear Equations

Now that you have reviewed slope, you are ready to graph linear equations. In this review lesson, you will focus on the following.

  • Graphing using the slope intercept form (y = mx + b) of the line.

  • Graphing vertical and horizontal lines.

  • Converting equations written in standard form (Ax + By = C) to slope intercept form and then graphing them.


Graphing linear equations in the form y = mx + b.

When an equation is written in slope intercept form, y = mx +b, what do m and b represent?

  • m represents the slope of the line

  • b represents the place where the line crosses the y-axis, called the y-intercept

You can graph linear equations by simply knowing the values of m and b.

Graphing horizontal and vertical lines.

As we reviewed earlier, horizontal and vertical lines have special equations.

  • Examples of Horizontal Lines: (Notice there are no x values in the equations.)
    • y = -2
    • y = 3
    • y = 0
  • Examples of Vertical Lines: (Notice there are no y values in the equations.)
    • x = 4
    • x = 0
    • x = -3

Now, let's graph these equations.

To graph a horizontal line, you simply draw a horizontal line that intersects the y axis at the number given in the equation. For example, in y = -2, the line intersects the y axis at -2.

To graph a vertical line, you simply draw a vertical line that intersects the x axis at the number given in the equation. For example, in x = 4, the line intersects the x axis at 4.

Changing equations from standard form to slope intercept form.

In this module, we will always convert our equations given in standard form to slope intercept form before graphing. This is a good practice because we can easily find the slope and y intercept when the equation is in the slope intercept form. This makes graphing a breeze!

  • Standard Form: Ax + By = C, where A, B and C are integers (no fractions or decimals).

  • Slope Intercept Form: y = mx + b, where m is the slope and b is the y-intercept of the line.


Example:

Write the equation in slope intercept form:

2x + 3y = 6


                 

Step 1: Determine what needs to be moved so the y is on one side of the equation by itself. Move the x term (2x in this case) to the right side of the equation.

To move this term, you will need to subtract 2x from both sides of the equation.

Because we are trying to write the equation in slope intercept form, the "x term" should be put immediately after the = sign on the right side.

Step 2: We now have 3y = -2x+6, but we want y, not 3y. Divide both sides of the equation by the 3 in the 3y term.

Second example: There should be a negative sign in front of the 3 ½. "Notice the y-intercept is -7/2 or -3.5 or -3 1/2."


Step 3: Simplify

Now the equation is in slope intercept form.

To graph the equation use the y-intercept, which is 2, and the slope, which is -2/3.

The slope tells us to move down 2 units and to the right 3 units.

Example:

Write the equation in slope intercept form:

4x - 2y = 7

 

Step 1: Again – Isolate the y term or get y by itself on one side of the equation. Move the x term (4x in this case) to the right side of the equation by subtracting 4x from each side.

Step 2: Divide both sides of the equation by the -2 in the -2y term. We now have -2y = -4x + 7. We want y so we divide by -2.

Important note: Be careful when there is a subtraction sign in front of the y term. Subtracting the 4x from both sides leaves us with -2y on the left side, not +2y. This is an error that students often make.

Step 3: Simplify

Now the equation is in slope-intercept form.

 

 

To graph the equation use the y-intercept, which is -7/2 or -3.5, and the slope, which is 2.

Notice the y-intercept is -7/2 or -3.5 or 3 1/2. How would you graph this value?

-3.5 is half way between -3 and -4. So, your y-intercept would be graphed between these two values on the y-axis.

The slope is 2 which means you count up 2 and over 1. You will have to stay in the spaces between the numbers on the y-axis because the y-intercept was graphed in the space between -3 and -4.
