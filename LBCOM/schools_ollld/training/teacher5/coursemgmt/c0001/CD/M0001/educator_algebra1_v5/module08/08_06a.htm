08.06 Solving Equations with Rational Expressions

When you are sick, sometimes symptoms can be misleading. For instance, if you played tennis for the first time yesterday, and today you have a temperature, sore throat, achy muscles and a head ache, you may think you have the flu. If you did not have the achy muscles, you would think you had a cold instead. Thinking back, the tennis game could have caused the achy muscles, and be an extraneous symptom for the flu. Now you only have a temperature, sore throat and a head ache. This sounds more like a cold.

The same is true of solutions to rational equations. There can be extraneous solutions. They are caused by variables in the denominators. Any time there is a variable in the denominator, there is a possibility of answers that are not real solutions to the equation. All answers must be tested by substituting them in the denominators of the original equation. If the answer makes any denominator equal to zero, then it is not a solution. It is called an extraneous solution.
